Question 1a: Central Limit Theorem Demonstration¶

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that the sum (or average) of a large number of independent random variables will be approximately normally distributed, regardless of the original distribution — as long as the variables have finite mean and variance. Using the Monte Carlo simulation, we will demonstrate the CLT.

  • 10,000 trials
  • 100 (sum of 100 uniform random variables)
    • min=0
    • max=1
  • Variance of the uniform distribution is 1/12.

Provide:

  • A histogram of the results of the MC simulation
    • Include a density plot of a normal distribution with the appropriate mean and standard deviation
    • Include the mean and standard deviation of the Monte Carlo simulation.

Note! Before coding, calculate the theoretical mean and standard deviation analytically. Simulation results should closely match theory.

Probability Density Function (PDF) of the Normal Distribution¶

$$ f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left( -\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) $$

where $\mu$ is the mean and $\sigma$ is the standard deviation.

The expected mean of the sum of 100 uniform(0,1) variables is 100 * 0.5 = 50, and the expected standard deviation is sqrt(100 * (1/12)) ≈ 2.89 [Square root of the variance]. Histogram should center around 50.

For the sum $S = X_1 + X_2 + \dots + X_{100}$ where each $X_i \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$:

$$ \mu_S = 100 \times 0.5 = 50, \qquad \sigma_S = \sqrt{100 \times \frac{1}{12}} \approx 2.89 $$

Figure 1 demonstrates the Central Limit Theorem with Histogram and a Normal Density Overlay.

The simulated histogram will show a prominent bell shape centered near 50. (Bars/Bins in Figure 1) Overlaying the normal density curve $\mathcal{N}(50, 2.89^2)$ provides an excellent visual match. (Bell-shaped Line in Figure 1)

The calculated mean and standard deviation from the simulation should closely agree with the theoretical values, that will demonstrate the Central Limit Theorem.

Question 1b: Beta Distribution¶

If $\left\{X_1,\dots,X_n\right\}$ are independent and uniformly distributed with random variables with minimum 0 and maximum 1, then the distribution of the $k^{th}$ order statistic $X_{(k)}$ is a Beta distribution with parameters $\alpha = k$ and $\beta = n+1-k$.

Using the Monte Carlo simulation we will:

  • Simulate 10,000 trials
  • Generate 10 uniform random variables for each trials
  • Select the 3rd lowest value from each trial

    • Include the mean and standard deviation of the Monte Carlo simulation.

Note! Know what to extract from the simulation and how it maps to the theoretical distribution.

  • Verify the results against the known beta distribution properties.

Beta Distribution of the k-th Order Statistic¶

$$ X_{(k)} \sim \text{Beta}(k, n+1-k) $$

In this demonstration:

  • $n = 10$ uniform random variables per trial
  • Extract the 3rd lowest value → $k = 3$
  • Therefore, the theoretical distribution is $\text{Beta}(3, 8)$

What we are extracting: In each trial, we generate 10 uniform values, sort them, and take the value at position 3 (3rd smallest).

How I understand this:¶

  • We are generating 10 uniform random variables in each trial.
  • Theoretical distribution is $\text{Beta}(k, n+1-k)$
    • or $\text{Beta}(3, 10+1-3)$
    • finally $\text{Beta}(3, 8)$ Theoretical mean: $$ \mu = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta} = \frac{3}{3 + 8} = \frac{3}{11} \approx 0.2727 $$

Theoretical variance: $$ \sigma^2 = \frac{\alpha \beta}{(\alpha + \beta)^2 (\alpha + \beta + 1)} = \frac{3 \times 8}{(3 + 8)^2 (3 + 8 + 1)} = \frac{24}{121 \cdot 12} \approx 0.0165 $$ Theoretical standard deviation: $$ \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2} \approx 0.1285 $$ Figure 2 demonstrates the Beta Distribution with Histogram and a Beta Density Overlay.

Memoryless Property, Exponential Distribution, and the Poisson Process¶

The Poisson distribution models the number of times an event occurs over a fixed interval of time or space

When: -Events occur independently -No two events occur at the same instant -The expected rate of occurrence remains constant accross an interval -$\lambda$ is the average number of events per interval (Hanbo, 2026)

The exponential distribution models time between events in a Poisson process, assuming that the rate of occurrence remains constant over time (Hanbo, 2026).The exponential distribution has a memoryless property, which states that the inter-arrival time between events follows an exponential distribution, then the number of events in a fixed time follows the Poisson distribution.

  • If the time between events follows an Exponential distribution with $\lambda$,
  • then the number of events in a fixed time period, such as one day, follows a Poisson distribution with $\lambda$.

Data for simulation:¶

  • Rate $\lambda = 10$ customers per day
  • We simulate the time until each customer arrives using Exponential(rate=10)
  • We count how many customers arrive in one day (1 unit of time)
  • We repeat this for 10,000 days [seems excessive]

Question 3: Life Insurance Policy NPV Simulation¶

A life insurance company is pricing a new 20-year term life policy for 45-year-old male non-smokers. The time until death is modeled using a Weibull distribution with shape parameter $k = 4.5$ and scale parameter $\lambda = 39$ years. Policyholders pay a monthly premium of $115$. If the policyholder dies during the term, beneficiaries receive a lump-sum death benefit of $1,000,000 at the end of that month. Each month there is a 0.3% chance the policy lapses. The company’s cost of capital is 6.5% per year.

The Weibull Distribution¶

The Weibull distribution is widely used to model time-to-event data, such as time until failure or death (Hanbo, 2026). Its probability density function (PDF) is given by:

$$ f(x; k, \lambda) = \frac{k}{\lambda} \left( \frac{x}{\lambda} \right)^{k-1} \exp\left[ -\left( \frac{x}{\lambda} \right)^k \right], \quad x \geq 0 $$

where:

  • $k$ = shape parameter (controls how the hazard rate changes over time)
  • $\lambda$ = scale parameter (characteristic life)

Interpretation of Parameters¶

  • The shape parameter $k = 4.5 > 1$ indicates that the hazard (failure) rate increases over time. This is realistic for human mortality, as the risk of death rises with advancing age.
  • The scale parameter $\lambda = 39$ years is known as the characteristic life. It has a special mathematical property: approximately 63.2% of individuals are expected to die by time $x = \lambda$.

The Scale Parameter and the Constant $e$¶

The cumulative distribution function (CDF) of the Weibull distribution at $x = \lambda$ is:

$$ F(\lambda) = 1 - \exp\left[ -\left( \frac{\lambda}{\lambda} \right)^k \right] = 1 - e^{-1} \approx 0.6321 \quad (63.21\%) $$

The constant $e \approx 2.71828$ is Euler’s number. The value $e^{-1} \approx 0.367879$ appears naturally in many continuous probability models. Therefore, by age $45 + 39 = 84$, approximately 63.2% of policyholders are expected to have died.

Specific PDF for this problem:

$$ f(x; 4.5, 39) = \frac{4.5}{39} \left( \frac{x}{39} \right)^{3.5} \exp\left[ -\left( \frac{x}{39} \right)^{4.5} \right] $$

Life Insurance Policy NPV Simulation¶

We model the net present value (NPV) of a 20-year term life insurance policy for a 45-year-old male non-smoker.

Considerations:

  • Time until death ~ Weibull(shape = 4.5, scale = 39 years)
  • Monthly premium = $115
  • Death benefit = $1,000,000 (paid at end of month of death)
  • Monthly lapse probability = 0.3% (0.003)
  • Annual discount rate = 6.5% (monthly rate = 6.5%/12)

For each simulated policyholder, we track monthly cash flows until one of the following occurs:

  • Policy lapses
  • Policyholder dies
  • Policy reaches 20 years (240 months)

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns
from scipy.stats import weibull_min

# ====================== LIFE INSURANCE NPV SIMULATION ======================
np.random.seed(42)
n_trials = 10_000
months = 240                          # 20 years × 12
monthly_premium = 115
death_benefit = 1_000_000
monthly_lapse_prob = 0.003
annual_discount_rate = 0.065
monthly_discount_rate = (1 + annual_discount_rate)**(1/12) - 1


npvs = np.zeros(n_trials)

for trial in range(n_trials):
    # Generate time until death (in years)
    death_time_years = weibull_min.rvs(c=4.5, scale=39)  # Reminder to self: rvs is the method to generate random variates from the distribution.
    death_month = int(np.ceil(death_time_years * 12))   # month in which death occurs
    
    npv = 0.0
    active = True
    month = 1
    
    while active and month <= months:
        # Discount factor for cash flows at end of month
        discount_factor = (1 + monthly_discount_rate) ** (-month)
        
        # Receive premium at the beginning of the month
        npv += monthly_premium * discount_factor
        
        # Check for lapse this month
        if np.random.rand() < monthly_lapse_prob:
            active = False
            break
        
        # Check if policyholder dies this month
        if month == death_month:
            npv -= death_benefit * discount_factor
            active = False
            break
        
        month += 1
    
    npvs[trial] = npv

# ====================== SUMMARY STATISTICS ======================
mean_npv = npvs.mean()
std_npv = npvs.std()
prob_profit = (npvs > 0).mean()

print("=== SINGLE POLICYHOLDER NPV RESULTS (10,000 trials) ===")
print(f"Mean NPV              : ${mean_npv:,.2f}")
print(f"Standard Deviation    : ${std_npv:,.2f}")
print(f"Probability of Profit : {prob_profit:.1%}")
print(f"Minimum NPV           : ${npvs.min():,.2f}")
print(f"Maximum NPV           : ${npvs.max():,.2f}")

Required Number of Trials for a 99% Confidence Interval with Half-Width of $200¶

The formula for the required sample size to achieve a desired margin of error is:

$$ n = \left( \frac{z_{\alpha/2} \cdot s}{E} \right)^2 $$

where:

  • $z_{\alpha/2}$ = z-score for 99% confidence = 2.576 (two-tailed)
  • $s$ = sample standard deviation = $58,810.56
  • $E$ = desired half-width (margin of error) = $200

Calculation:

$$ n = \left( \frac{2.576 \times 58,810.56}{200} \right)^2 \approx 573,700 $$

Interpretation:
Due to the high variability in NPV (mainly caused by the occasional large death benefit payout), the insurance company would need approximately 573,700 simulation trials to estimate the mean NPV with a margin of error of ±$200 at the 99% confidence level.

Required Number of Trials for a 99% Confidence Interval with Half-Width of $200¶

Due to diversification, far fewer portfolio simulations are needed to achieve tight confidence intervals compared to analyzing policies individually.
