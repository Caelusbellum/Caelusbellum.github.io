Question 1¶

GWS is a company that markets outboard motorboats directly to consumers for recreational use. Recently, they’ve been developing a project they think has a lot of potential: the first mass market boats with electric motors. They haven’t started advertising their new product yet, nor have they organized a presale because they don’t want to lose their first-mover advantage. As a result, GWS has a limited understanding of the size of the market for their new project. They plan to retail their boats for $150,000, but after two years, when competition enters the market and the novelty factor wears off, they’ll have to drop the price to $70,000, and they will be eventually sold at $70,000. They hire a consultant who estimates that at this price point, over the next two years, demand for the new boats will be somewhere between 2,000 and 15,000, with probabilities as in the Table 1 below:

Demand Probability
2,000-5,000 35%
5,001-10,000 40%
10,001-14,000 20%
14,001-15,000 5%

Table 1: Discrete Distribution of Demand for GWS Electric Boats

The fixed cost of manufacturing any number of boats is normally distributed, with a mean of $300 million and a standard deviation of $60 million. They estimate that the variable cost to produce one boat will be a minimum of $77 thousand and a maximum of $100 thousand, with a most likely value of $90,000. Develop four Monte Carlo Simulations to calculate their total profit over the two year period assuming they produce a. 4,000 boats b. 8,000 boats c. 12,000 boats d. 15,000 boats For each simulation, provide the mean and standard deviation as well as a histogram.

Problem Statement¶

GWS is launching electric motorboats

  • First 2 years: $150,000 per boat
  • After 2 years: $70,000 per boat (due to competition)

Uncertain Variables:

  • Demand over 2 Years looks like discrete distribution (competitive advantage)
  • Fixed Cost ~ Normal Distribution
    • $\mu$ = $300M,
    • $\sigma$ = $60M
  • Variable Cost ~ Triangular Distribution
    • min = $77k
    • mode = $90k
    • max = $100k

Monte Carlo Simulations: Run 10,000 simulations at 4,000, 8,000, 12,000, and 15,000 boats to estimate total profit, and analyze results (mean, std dev, histogram).

What is the expected profit for each production level, and what is the best production, if any, based on the simulations?

Generating Demand (Discrete Distribtion)¶

The consultant's estimates for demand can be represented as a discrete distribution. Try using the probabilities according to Table 1 to generate random demand values for each simulation.

Generating the Costs¶

Fixed Cost (one-time manufacturing setup cost):
$$ \text{FixedCost}_i \sim \text{Normal}(\mu = 300,000,000,\ \sigma = 60,000,000) $$

Variable Cost per boat:
$$ \text{VarCost}_i \sim \text{Triangular}(a = 77,000,\ c = 90,000,\ b = 100,000) $$

where:

  • $a$ = minimum value ($77,000)
  • $c$ = mode (most likely value = $90,000)
  • $b$ = maximum value ($100,000)
  • $i$ = simulation trial number

The total variable cost for producing $Q$ boats in simulation $i$ looks like: $$ \text{TotalVarCost}_i = Q \times \text{VarCost}_i $$

Calculating Profit, Sales, and Revenue¶

For the production of $Q$ boats with demand $D_i$ in simulation $i$, calculate revenue for the first 2 years; then additional produced after the first 2 years that are sold at the lower price.

  • Sell as many as possible at $150,000 during the first 2 years: $$ \text{RevenueFirst2YearsHigh}_i = \min(Q, D_i) \times 150,000 $$
  • After two years of production, sell the remaining demand at $70,000: $$ \text{RevenueAfter2YearsLow}_i = \max(0, D_i - Q) \times 70,000 $$

Total Revenue: $$ \text{TotalRevenue}_i = \text{RevenueFirst2YearsHigh}_i + \text{RevenueAfter2YearsLow}_i $$

Profit per Simulation:¶

For each simulation $i$, calculate profit as: $$ \text{Profit}_i = \text{TotalRevenue}_i - \text{FixedCost}_i - \text{TotalVarCost}_i $$ Run 10,000 simulations for each production level (4,000, 8,000, 12,000, and 15,000 boats) to estimate total profit, and analyze results (mean, std dev, histogram).

Analyzing Results for Decision-Making Recommendations¶

We are looking for the expected profit for each production level as well as the best production level based on the simulations. This will drive the decision on how many boats to produce, or not produce according to the consultant's estimates of demand and probability (Table 1).

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

np.random.seed(42)  # For reproducibility
n_trials = 10_000

# Demand distribution
demand_ranges = [(2000, 5000), (5001, 10_000), (10_001, 14_000), (14_001, 15_000)]
demand_probabilities = [0.35, 0.40, 0.20, 0.05]

def simulate_profit(quantity):
    profits = np.zeros(n_trials)
    
    for i in range(n_trials):
        # Generate demand
        demand_bin = np.random.choice(4, p=demand_probabilities)
        low, high = demand_ranges[demand_bin]
        demand = np.random.randint(low, high + 1)
        
        # Generate the Costs
        fixed_cost = np.random.normal(300_000_000, 60_000_000)
        variable_cost = np.random.triangular(77_000, 90_000, 100_000, n_trials)[i]
        
        # Calculate Sales and Revenue
        sold_at_high_price = min(quantity, demand)  # First 2 years at $150k
        sold_at_low_price = max(0, quantity - demand)  #fixed.  Originally was demand - quantity, which could be negative if demand < quantity.
        
        revenue = sold_at_high_price * 150_000 + sold_at_low_price * 70_000
        total_variable_cost = quantity * variable_cost
        
        profit = revenue - fixed_cost - total_variable_cost
        profits[i] = profit
        
    return profits
        
# Run the simulations for all quantities
quantities = [4000, 8000, 12000, 15000]
results = {}

for q in quantities:
    results[q] = simulate_profit(q)
    print(f"Completed simulation for {q:,} boats")

Part (b): Chi-squared Test for Goodness of Fit / Shifting the Distribution¶

For discrete distributions, we need to use Chi-squared goodness of fit testing to determine which distribution best fits the data.
The Chi-squared test compares the observed frequencies of party sizes to the expected frequencies under each distribution. The test statistic is calculated as: $$ \chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i} $$ where $O_i$ is the observed frequency for category $i$, $E_i$ is the expected frequency for category $i$, and $k$ is the number of categories.

Below is a Python code that performs the Chi-squared goodness of fit test to determine which distribution best fits the party size data.

In [22]:

shift = min_size - 1  # Shift to start at 1

print(f"Minimum party size: {min_size}")
print(f"Shift applied     : {shift}\n")

observed_freq = party_size.value_counts().sort_index()

x = observed.index.values

# Prepare bins for testing the expected frequencies

# === Shifted Poisson Distribution ===
# Estimate lambda (mean) for the shifted Poisson
poisson_lambda = party_size.mean() - shift
expected_poisson = stats.poisson.pmf(bins[:-1] - shift, mu=poisson_lambda) * len(party_size)

# === Shifted Geometric Distribution ===
geom_p = 1 / (party_size.mean() - shift + 1)  # p = 1/(mean + 1) for shifted geometric
expected_geom = stats.geom.pmf(bins[:-1] - shift, p=geom_p) * len(party_size)

# Chi-squared tests
print("=== Goodness of Fit Test ===")

chi2_p, p_poisson = stats.chisquare(observed_freq, expected_poisson * (observed.sum() / expected_poisson.sum()))
chi2_geom, p_geom = stats.chisquare(observed_freq, expected_geom * (observed.sum() / expected_geom.sum()))

print(f"Shifted Poisson (lambda={poisson_lambda:.2f}): Chi-squared = {chi2_p:.3f}, p-value = {p_poisson:.4f}")
print(f"Shifted Geometric (p={geom_p:.4f}): Chi-squared = {chi2_geom:.3f}, p-value = {p_geom:.4f}")

Part (c): Distribution Fit for Revenue Per Person Spending¶

To determine which distribution best fits the revenue per person spending data, we can use the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test. The KS test compares the empirical distribution of the data to a specified theoretical distribution and calculates a statistic that measures the distance between the two distributions. The distribution with the smallest KS statistic is considered the best fit for the data. Below is a Python code that performs the KS test for the specified distributions.

Part (f): Monte Carlo Simulation for Revenue Per Event¶

Using the distributions and parameters from parts (a)-(c) and the correlation from part e, we can construct a Monte Carlo simulation with 100,000 trials to create a risk profile for the revenue generated from a single event. Below is a Python code that performs this simulation and creates a histogram of the results, along with the mean and standard deviation of the simulated revenues.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns
from scipy.stats import poisson, lognorm

np.random.seed(42)
n_trials = 100_000

# Parameters from earlier fitting
shift = 12                                 # From min party size = 13
poisson_lambda = 7.84                      # Shifted Poisson parameter
spending_params = lognorm.fit(df['rev_per_person'])   # Best fit for spending

correlation = 0.5088                       # From Spearman correlation

revenues = np.zeros(n_trials)

for i in range(n_trials):
    # Generate correlated uniforms
    u1 = np.random.rand()
    u2 = stats.norm.cdf(
        stats.norm.ppf(u1) * correlation + 
        stats.norm.ppf(np.random.rand()) * np.sqrt(1 - correlation**2)
    )
    
    # Party Size (Shifted Poisson → shift back to original scale)
    party_size_sim = poisson.ppf(u1, mu=poisson_lambda) + shift   # ← Shift back!
    
    # Per Person Spending (Lognormal)
    spend_sim = lognorm.ppf(u2, *spending_params)
    
    # Total Revenue
    revenues[i] = party_size_sim * spend_sim

# ====================== RESULTS ======================
print("=== REVENUE SIMULATION RESULTS (100,000 trials) ===")
print(f"Mean Revenue    : ${revenues.mean():,.2f}")
print(f"Std Dev Revenue : ${revenues.std():,.2f}")
print(f"Min Revenue     : ${revenues.min():,.2f}")
print(f"Max Revenue     : ${revenues.max():,.2f}")
print(f"P(Revenue ≥ $5000): {(revenues >= 5000).mean():.1%}")

Part (g)-(h): Probability of Generating at Least $5000 and Comparison of Correlated and Independent Simulations¶

To calculate the probability of generating at least $5000 from a private event, we can count the number of simulated revenues that are greater than or equal to $5000 and divide by the total number of simulations (100,000). Below is a Python code that performs this calculation for the correlated and independent simulations.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns
from scipy.stats import poisson, lognorm

np.random.seed(42)
n_trials = 100_000

# Parameters from fitting
shift = 12
poisson_lambda = 7.84
spending_params = lognorm.fit(df['rev_per_person'])
correlation = 0.5088

# ====================== CORRELATED SIMULATION ======================
revenues_correlated = np.zeros(n_trials)

for i in range(n_trials):
    u1 = np.random.rand()
    u2 = stats.norm.cdf(stats.norm.ppf(u1) * correlation + 
                       stats.norm.ppf(np.random.rand()) * np.sqrt(1 - correlation**2))
    
    party_size_sim = poisson.ppf(u1, mu=poisson_lambda) + shift   # Shift back!
    spend_sim = lognorm.ppf(u2, *spending_params)
    
    revenues_correlated[i] = party_size_sim * spend_sim

# ====================== INDEPENDENT SIMULATION ======================
revenues_independent = np.zeros(n_trials)

for i in range(n_trials):
    party_size_sim = poisson.ppf(np.random.rand(), mu=poisson_lambda) + shift
    spend_sim = lognorm.ppf(np.random.rand(), *spending_params)
    revenues_independent[i] = party_size_sim * spend_sim

# ====================== RESULTS ======================
print("=== REVENUE SIMULATION RESULTS (100,000 trials) ===")
print(f"Correlated Mean Revenue   : ${revenues_correlated.mean():,.2f}")
print(f"Independent Mean Revenue  : ${revenues_independent.mean():,.2f}")
print(f"Correlated Std Dev        : ${revenues_correlated.std():,.2f}")
print(f"Independent Std Dev       : ${revenues_independent.std():,.2f}")
print(f"\nP(Revenue ≥ $5000 | Correlated)   : {(revenues_correlated >= 5000).mean():.1%}")
print(f"P(Revenue ≥ $5000 | Independent) : {(revenues_independent >= 5000).mean():.1%}")

Results of Correlated vs. Independent Simulations¶

In this scenario, the difference in mean revenue between the correlated and independent simulations is not significant, but the probability of generating at least $5000 is higher in the correlated simulation (11.3%) compared to the independent simulation (7.3%). This suggests that accounting for the correlation between party size and per person spending can lead to a more accurate estimation of the revenue generated from private events, and it may indicate a higher likelihood of achieving higher revenues when considering the correlation.

This means that by analyzing the correlation, there seems to be a benefit in terms of revenue estimation and risk assessment for private events at the Baker's Inn.
